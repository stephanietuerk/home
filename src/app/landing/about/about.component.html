about

I design and build systems, primarily in code, that turn complex data into useful and intelligible tools. I love working at the intersection of structure, meaning, and interaction, and translating between quantitative data, visual representation, and language. This work draws on my training as an architect, historian, and engineer.

Currently, I lead design and frontend development on high-complexity, data-heavy public-sector applications at Mathematica, a quantitative public policy research firm. I joined in 2019 as the company’s first design-focused hire on the web team, and since then I’ve grown and led the design practice, served as the frontend lead for our most demanding web-based deliverables, and mentored both designers and engineers.

My work includes:

  • Architecting frontend systems that model and handle complex, sensitive, and incomplete data with nuance, clarity, and precision
  • Designing UI component and data visualization libraries that balance developer experience, accessibility, and architectural flexibility
  • Owning the end-to-end user experience of high-stakes deliverables—shaping structure, flows, language, and visual hierarchy from early-stage client conversations through to design and implementation.

Before moving into data visualization and interfaces in 2017, I spent many years in the field of architecture (M.Arch and PhD in the History, Theory, and Criticism of Architecture and Art), and my current work is deeply informed by that background. I believe that complex information is best made legible to users—whether end-users of an application or developers using tools or reading code—through consistent and systematic application of design logic. I approach interface and system design as part of a continued practice of assembling evidence, framing interpretation, and constructing meaning in and for the public.
