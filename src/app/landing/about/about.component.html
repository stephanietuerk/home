about

I design and build systems, primarily in code, that turn complex data into useful and intelligible tools. I love working at the intersection of structure, meaning, and interaction, and translating between quantitative data, language, and visual representation. This work draws on my training as an architect, historian, and engineer.

Currently, I lead design and frontend development on high-complexity, data-heavy public-sector applications at Mathematica, a quantitative public policy research firm. I joined in 2019 as the company’s first design-focused hire on the web team. Since then I’ve grown and led the design practice, served as the frontend lead for our most demanding web-based deliverables, and mentored both designers and engineers.

My work includes:

  • Architecting frontend systems that model and handle structurally complex, semantically rich, and intricate data with clarity and precision
  • Designing data visualization and UI component libraries that balance developer experience, accessibility, and architectural flexibility
  • Owning the end-to-end experience and delivery of high-stakes deliverables—shaping structure, flows, language, and visual hierarchy from early-stage client conversations through product launch and ongoing enhancements.

Before moving into data visualization and interface work in 2017, I spent many years in the field of architecture (M.Arch and PhD in the History, Theory, and Criticism of Architecture and Art). My current work designing interfaces, systems, and developer tools builds on that background in both method and focus. I approach system and interface design as a continuation of longstanding lines of inquiry: how to construct meaning from complexity, how to organize evidence for an audience, and how to make that meaning legible through the development of consistent and coherent systems of design.
