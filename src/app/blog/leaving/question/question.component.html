There are some things I would never tell people to do—for example, "you absolutely should (or shouldn't) leave academia"—but there is one thing that I tell everyone to do without hesitation and with a lot of conviction:

Talk to anyone you can talk to.

I would recommend starting by asking everyone you know if they know anyone who was in a remotely similar situation/field as yours who has left academia and ask if they could put you in touch. Do not turn down any offered connection / conversation down. Maybe you are like, "I'm a comp lit person and my friend offered to put me in touch with their friend who left academia with a CS PhD and now is at Google—that's not going to be applicable!" Wrong, talk to that person. If someone offers a conversation with someone in an industry you are completely opposed to working in, take them up on their offer.

Why do I think this is so important? My experience is that it is extremely difficult to understand the landscape of jobs, roles, and organizations outside of academia from within it. And if you don't know that things exist, you can't want to do them, apply to them, be knowledgeable enough to have a successful interview, and so forth.

If you don't know what you would ask in these kinds of conversations, here are some ideas.

For people who left a similar situation as yours:

  • How did you get interested in your current field?
  • How did you get your first job in your current field?
  • What was the most important thing you did in terms of finding new opportunities?
  • What was the biggest surprise for you in leaving academia to do something else?

For everyone:

  • Can you give me a high-level overview of the landscape of your field/industry? (i.e. a specific non-profit sector, a sector of tech, tech at large, consulting, federal government, think-tanks, etc.) What is the job market like in it?
  • Generally, what roles are in your organization? Are there roles that would be a good fit (i.e. they would hire me, I would find it engaging) for someone like me if I had a bit of additional training and experience that I could realistically get in the next year or two? [Describe what you think you are good at, any special skills that may be relevant, and what you think you may be good at with training/experience.]
  • Which of these roles are roles that exist across your field/industry?
  • Are there any growth/new areas in your industry where there is a lot of demand for employees but no formal educational path to hire from? (Note: I would never advocate going into a job strictly based on demand, but I think this is useful information to know, as these are areas where people may be more likely to hire someone who is "generally smart.")
  • What do you like about your industry? What do you like about your particular job?
  • What is the worst part of your industry? What is the worst part of your job?
  • Do you know anyone who you think it would be helpful for me to talk with further? (i.e. someone with similar experiences, interests, or background to mine)

Things to do:

  • Prioritize your own happiness and well-being, and get real about what will really make you happy in the future.
  • To the greatest extent possible, free yourself from any professional expectations you have for yourself or that you imagine others may have of you. This is not the time to care about what your academic peers and mentors may think of you.
  • Be extremely open minded about what the future may hold for you. Keep as many options open as possible.
  • Make a list of things that would make you happy in a job and let that guide your search.
  • Talk to everyone you can, and take advantage of every resource you can. It is very hard to know much about the landscape of jobs outside of academia from within it, so you will need to do research. Learning that something is wrong for you is just as valuable as learning that something is appealing.
  • Don't set out to shift into a field/profession simply because there are jobs. Do look for opportunities to gain exposure to new skills that will bring value to any job.
  • Don't let fear of failure hold you back from applying to things or interviewing. You will learn a lot from going through the process, even if it doesn't result in a job.

Things to keep in mind:

  • Getting your first job in a non-scholarly role is by far the biggest challenge. After you have some experience, you can much more easily find a new position, and even shift into a quite different role. What you do next may be great, or it may be a stepping stone to something else a year later.
  • Statistically your odds of getting a job outside of academia are much, much, much higher than getting one inside. You will eventually find something.
  • Most work environments outside of academia are far more supportive, nurturing, and reasonable than academic environments.
  • You are a capable, curious person, and will bring value to the world no matter what you do next. I know that sounds cheesy, but it is true, and easy to forget.
